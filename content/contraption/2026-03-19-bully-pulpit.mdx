In January 2020 as [Moonlight](/moonlight-s-pitch-deck) was acquired, I began setting up [The Contraption Company](/introducing-contraption-co) LLC in New York State. The process was a bit involved, including requirements to publish announcements in two physical newspapers. My vision was to create a company in search of a product. The hardest part about selling Moonlight was losing my bully pulpit: the newsletter list I had built, which let me test new features and get quick feedback. Inspired by 37Signals, I thought that creating the company independent of the products could let me experiment while still growing that mailing list. There was a bit of recognition of [code as content](/code-as-content-and-digital-proprioception) here, that creators were coming to software and throwaway projects that gained followers, like that by Pieter Levels, was accepted.

I ended up not working full-time on Contraption Company until 2022 when I left Webflow, doing a mix of consulting and my own projects.

As I joined Find AI as a founder, then went full-time on the engineering team at [Chroma](/chroma), I stopped working as much on Contraption as a product studio, and converted it to more of a hobby blog. And, it has honestly been really rewarding. But, at the same time, AI was enabling the vision I had come up with, of quickly building many interesting products. So, I had a lot of fun last year building projects like [a Mac Mini home server](/a-mini-data-center), [Trivet](/trivet-adds-google-sign-in-to-ghost), and [a print edition](/introducing-the-print-edition).

Coming into 2026, I have enjoyed the pattern of writing about my work (especially because I do not use social media). But, two things have become clear: one is that I have my identity splintered across multiple sites, [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com) for a personal "what I am up to" newsletter, and [contraption.co](https://contraption.co) for two newsletters, an eponymous newsletter about projects and essays, and [Workshop](/introducing-workshop) about work in progress. And, the second thing is that Ghost is hard to edit.

As I have been building recently, I have been building tools for myself. [Toolbox](/how-to-host-web-apps-on-a-mac-mini) for hosting apps on a Mac Mini. Most recently Trivet for adding Google sign-in to Ghost. I originally built on Ghost because it mixed list management and writing in a nice way. But over time it has felt more clunky. I set up [Three-Ring Binder](/three-ring-binder) to mix AI into my writing, and that got me really excited about local files.

The straw that broke the camel's back is that I was really inspired by the [Mintlify case study](https://www.trychroma.com/customers/mintlify-case-study) we published at Chroma about using SPLADE on Chroma Cloud for typeahead search. We switched Chroma's docs to Mintlify, and I really like the Mintlify UX, including using an agent for deep research on docs that can even write code for you. After going from "really good typeahead search with an agent" to other sites, it feels old-school. So, I wanted to bring that better search experience to [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com). I set out doing it on Ghost, and struggled, and realized it would not automatically be available on [Postcard](https://postcard.page)-powered [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com).

So, I started a new project, [Bully Pulpit](https://github.com/philipithomas/bully-pulpit), an attempt to merge my website into one, and rely entirely on local files instead of a CMS. This was based on a couple of things. As I built the Three-Ring Binder project, I realized how powerful search over local files could be. The second was that Ghost's split between theme, server, and data made it hard to develop on real data because it was so spread out; it was hard for me, but also hard for coding agents. Whereas having everything in one repo meant I could see my theme and my content all together, and I do not need to do things like give agents access to databases to query data. I built it in Next.js, which also let me incorporate advanced UI like typeahead search really cleanly, while incorporating many practices to make the site [really fast](https://craigmod.com/essays/fast_software/).

This overall fits into my recent thinking about the future of software, that it is moving from something people buy to something people build themselves. I think of this quote I put in my [January newsletter](/craft-in-the-digital-era) often from [New Nordic Cuisine, Aesthetics, and Place: A Compendium](https://petermiller.com/products/new-nordic-cuisine-aesthetics-and-place-a-compendium-new-exhibit-arriving-in-seattle-november-15th):

> In many ways, the closing decades of the 21st century were all about having more of everything, a period marked by an aesthetics of abundance. In the private home, kitchens grew bigger and glistened with chrome and cool surfaces. Since then, things have changed. The glossy sheen of the mass-produced gave way to a desire for something warmer, for objects that radiate individuality, history and a sense of place.

The software parallel here is that we just went through an era where everybody went from no software to software everywhere, and they could buy CRMs, VMSs, editors, and more. And, now we are moving from the era of mass-produced software to customized software, where there will still be more software, but it will now be more in-house and customized, so instead of shopping for software to solve a workflow problem, they are more likely to try building it themselves.

My journey of building my personal website was driven by this. AI was so good that I could write requirements and build my own custom site. I also found myself rethinking many paradigms; in generic software you always think in terms of a database and multiple users. But, the local filesystem approach was much more germane to single-person software. It simplifies life, and also enables me to use GitHub Actions for flows like syncing posts to Chroma, which would historically require much more advanced controllers. No [complex backup systems](/backup-plans). So, by thinking "just me" you can make software simpler and cleaner.

Finally, in the path to updating the UI, I needed to build a way to manage subscribers and emails. So I built an additional project called [Printing Press](https://github.com/philipithomas/printing-press) which runs on my Mac Mini and can manage subscribers and emails. I liked that I could continue to lean my custom quirkiness into here, such as native support for multiple newsletters and custom fonts. Also inspired by AI, I made no UI for Printing Press and built a command line for it. (Even here, I had some interesting challenges. To prevent AI agents from triggering emails in production, I tried some different techniques, but settled on tying destructive actions to the 1Password CLI, so I would have to scan my fingerprint to get emails to send in production, making the CLI safer against agentic mess-ups.) I built the entire thing in Rust because I am [using it at Chroma now](/sync-phone-numbers-and-webhooks) and I find that building things myself is how I learn.

So, today I am retiring the Contraption Company brand, and merging the domain [contraption.co](https://contraption.co) here into [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com). My emails in the future will come from mail@philipithomas.com instead of philip@contraption.co.

I have kept three newsletters:
- Postcard is my old [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com) posts, monthly posts about what I am up to
- Contraption is a newsletter about projects and essays
- Workshop is a journal about work in progress

So Contraption Company name is retired, but I will call the newsletter Contraption for posterity. Also, [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com) is no longer powered by the [Postcard](https://github.com/contraptionco/postcard) software, but I am keeping that name for the monthly updates newsletter; more on that soon. **If you subscribed to one site, you are not automatically subscribed to all three newsletters. Contraption subscribers are not getting the Postcard newsletter by default.** To manage that, you can [manage your account here](https://www.philipithomas.com/account).

Finally, my favorite thing about the entire new setup is that I incorporated an AI agent into the site, powered by Chroma. I used the same [Bell name and icon from my OpenClaw](/openclaw-is-my-new-coworker). But, now on any page on the site, you can ask questions like "What craft-focused books are mentioned" or "what programming languages does Philip use" and get answers that span multiple posts. I find myself the main user of this, to search my [years of "What I am up to" posts](https://www.philipithomas.com/postcard) semantically, for simple things like "what coffee shops did I like in Stockholm" that traditional search just does not do a good job of.

So, come try out the site. I am proud of the search functionality and agentic search; play around with that. And, it is all [open-source](https://github.com/philipithomas/bully-pulpit). Let me know what you think or if you find any bugs.
