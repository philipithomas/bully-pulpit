In [Spring cleaning](/spring-cleaning), I wrote that I had some downtime between jobs and wanted to redirect more of my energy toward creative projects here. I meant that as a software note, but it also created space for a different kind of practice.

This week, I got a new camera and flew to Japan alone for two weeks. I have been to Japan before with loose interests in coffee, craft, and infrastructure. This trip has a simpler intention: practice photography every day, notice what I miss when I am moving too quickly, and get better by making more pictures than I know what to do with.

Normally, my photos live inside essays. A photo of a bridge becomes a cover image for a post about AI agents. A cafe table becomes an illustration for a note about newsletters or software. That has worked well for years, but it also means many photos wait around for a future essay that may never exist.

That stack reminded me of [tsundoku](/diction): acquiring books and letting them pile up unread. I am guilty of the book version, too. On this trip, I am making a photo version: a pile of images waiting for a home.

So, today I am introducing [Tsundoku](/tsundoku), a pop-up photography newsletter from this trip.

The format is inspired by [Craig Mod's pop-up newsletters](https://craigmod.com/essays/popup_newsletters/): a temporary publication with a clear beginning and end. Tsundoku will run for about two weeks, then stop when the trip is over. No growth strategy, no content calendar, no permanent obligation. Just a small container for practice.

I also wanted the container to feel special. [Luis Mendo](https://www.luismendo.com/) helped me with the wordmark and logo, set in his proprietary and secret typeface Mendo Titles Bold. I love his work, and you should also read [Mundo Mendo](https://www.mundomendo.com/), his blog about illustration, cities, books, and life in Tokyo.

If you already subscribe to *Contraption*, I am not automatically adding you to *Tsundoku*. If you want to follow along, [go to the Tsundoku page](/tsundoku) and enter your email. Expect several photos per day for the next two weeks, then no emails at all afterward. You can also ignore email entirely and just check the page, where I will add each photo as I publish it.

The project also ties back to the point of Spring cleaning. I wrote there that I wanted to move away from projects that are software for other people and toward creative work I can build for myself. Tsundoku is both: a photography project, and a small piece of software made to support that project.

I finished most of the infrastructure and UI today on the Shinkansen from Kanazawa to Tokyo, as a typhoon moved through. The post pages, photo viewer, email templates, and subscription plumbing all run inside this site. It was also my first time using the [Codex desktop app for Mac](https://developers.openai.com/codex/app), and I was impressed. The whole implementation is open source in the [bully-pulpit repo](https://github.com/philipithomas/bully-pulpit).

I am trying to treat software as part of the creative process, not only as delivery infrastructure. This little newsletter is the first test.
