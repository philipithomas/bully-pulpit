After I sold [Moonlight](/moonlight-s-pitch-deck) in 2020, I started imagining asynchronous community software called Booklet. At Moonlight, we were building a community of remote software engineers. Slack was the core communication software, but over time I built a smart newsletter that used collaborative filtering to create a personalized digest for each member. When I finally looked at our analytics, I realized that the newsletter had ten times as many weekly active users as our website. I had thought of Moonlight as primarily a website, but for our community it was primarily a newsletter.

The power of a newsletter is that it is asynchronous - you can read it on your own time and get a summary of activity. Chat apps such as Slack tend to require [constant attention](/digital-quiet) to keep up with what is happening.

As I reflected on online community building, I wanted to build software for readers - something newsletter-first, for the majority of community members who just want to consume content. I wrote about this idea years later in [Community-powered media companies](/community-media-companies): online communities tend to evolve into something like a group Substack.

So, I started building Booklet in 2020, then returned to it in earnest in 2023. That September, I [launched FRCTNL](/introducing-frctnl), a Moonlight-inspired community on Booklet. Thirteen days later, I [launched Booklet](/launching-booklet) as a standalone product. I coded the entire product myself in [Ruby on Rails](/rails-versus-nextjs) (by hand, before AI coding tools), and later [wrote about its architecture](/booklet-architecture). I did use the then-new OpenAI APIs to power the original newsletter, with GPT-3 summarizing community conversations into a daily digest. I also [recorded myself coding a Booklet AI feature](/code-a-booklet-ai-feature) from idea to production.

Over the years, Booklet hosted various communities, including a venture capital fund's founder community and several small groups. But it never found sustained adoption beyond the communities I operated myself. FRCTNL continued to grow, yet running a free community increasingly meant [fighting spam](/sync-phone-numbers-and-webhooks); by the end, I saw more AI bots sign up than humans. I added [manual quarantine controls](/maintenance) in 2025, but decided to [shut down Booklet and FRCTNL](/spring-cleaning) earlier in 2026.

Still, small, purpose-built online communities are having a moment. Craig Mod built [The Good Place](https://craigmod.com/essays/memberships_year_seven/) for his membership program: a private social network with deliberately narrow rules, including posts that disappear after seven days and daily limits on posting. It has become one of the most successful parts of his membership. Charging for access also helps him keep bad actors out.

Booklet did not work as a broad, free software service. But its code may work better as owner-operated software shaped around the norms of a specific community. I think it is a great time to [build software for yourself](/app-of-ones-own), and a forum is a good place to start.

So, today I am open-sourcing the Booklet code. Like I did when I [open-sourced Postcard](/postcard-open-source), I set up the repository so you can run it in Solo mode as a single-tenant application or in Multiuser mode as a multi-tenant software service. Solo mode runs without billing, subdomains, or the internal administration panel. This is the production application, not a stripped-down demo: it includes member management, custom domains, moderation, a REST API, and the newsletter system that once sent to thousands of people each day. The MIT-licensed repository includes a Dockerfile and a Render blueprint for deployment with PostgreSQL.

Booklet also became a proving ground for search. It uses [Chroma Cloud](/chroma) for semantic search, with a separate collection for each community. Rebuilding that search eventually pushed me to publish [Chroma for Ruby](/chroma-for-ruby).

Some people may fork Booklet and modify it for their own communities. But my deeper hope is that more people reuse the newsletter AI techniques. [LLMs excel at summarization](/archetypes-of-llm-apps), and summaries create *legibility* for community members who are not always logging in.

One thing I learned while building Booklet is that it is better to send every member the same set of posts and discussions instead of a personalized digest. People who post frequently like to see their own posts in the newsletter, and a shared digest gives the entire community the same account of what is happening.

You can see [the code that summarizes each post and its replies](https://github.com/philipithomas/booklet/blob/main/app/services/new_post_summarization_service.rb), explore [the entire repository on GitHub](https://github.com/philipithomas/booklet), and let me know if you build anything cool with it.
