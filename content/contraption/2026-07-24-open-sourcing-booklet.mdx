While building [Moonlight](/moonlight-s-pitch-deck)'s developer community, I built a smart newsletter to create a personalized digest for each member. When I later looked at our analytics, I realized that the newsletter had ten times as many weekly active users as our website. I thought of the company as a web application, but for our community it was primarily a newsletter.

The power of a newsletter is that it is asynchronous - you can read it on your own time and get a summary of activity. Chat apps such as Slack tend to require [constant attention](/digital-quiet) to keep up with what is happening.

As I reflected on online community building, I wanted to build software for readers - something newsletter-first, for the majority of community members who just want to consume content. I detailed this idea later in [Community-powered media companies](/community-media-companies): online communities tend to evolve into something like a group Substack.

So, I started building Booklet as newsletter-first community software. In 2023, I [launched FRCTNL](/introducing-frctnl), a Moonlight-inspired community on Booklet. A couple of weeks later, I [launched Booklet](/launching-booklet) as a standalone product. I coded the entire application myself in [Ruby on Rails](/rails-versus-nextjs) (by hand!), and later [wrote about its architecture](/booklet-architecture). I did use the then-new OpenAI APIs to power the original newsletter, with GPT-3 summarizing community conversations into a daily digest.

Over the years, Booklet hosted various communities, including a venture capital fund's founder community and several small groups. But it never found sustained adoption beyond the communities I operated myself. FRCTNL continued to grow, yet running a free community increasingly meant [fighting spam](/sync-phone-numbers-and-webhooks); by the end, I saw more AI bots posting than humans. I decided to [shut down Booklet and FRCTNL](/spring-cleaning) last month.

So, today I am open-sourcing the Booklet code. Like I did when I [open-sourced Postcard](/postcard-open-source), I set up the repository so you can run it in a simple Solo mode as a single-tenant application, or in Multiuser mode as a multi-tenant software service with billing and multi-domain support. This is the production application, not a stripped-down demo. The MIT-licensed repository includes a Dockerfile and a Render blueprint for deployment with PostgreSQL.

Booklet did not work as a standalone product, but I had fun building it. I think it is a great time to [build software for yourself](/app-of-ones-own), so perhaps Booklet can have a second life among people who customize it for their own needs. (Small communities on custom software are having a moment - Craig Mod's [The Good Place](https://craigmod.com/essays/memberships_year_seven/), for example.)

My deeper hope from open-sourcing this product is that more people implement Booklet's newsletter AI techniques. [LLMs excel at summarization](/archetypes-of-llm-apps), and good newsletters create *legibility* for community members who are not always logging in. Remember - your newsletter probably has more users than your website, so spend time making it great<span id="fnref1" />[\[1\]](#fn1)! You can see [the code that summarizes each post and its replies](https://github.com/philipithomas/booklet/blob/main/app/services/new_post_summarization_service.rb) here.

[Check out Booklet on GitHub](https://github.com/philipithomas/booklet), and let me know if you build anything cool with it.
