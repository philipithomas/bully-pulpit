I have decided to retire the [Contraption Company](/introducing-contraption-co) brand, and merge the domain [contraption.co](https://contraption.co) here into [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com). Emails will come from this new domain.

The impetus was a desire to add some new features to the site, and I felt that maintaining two different sites was a bit overkill. So, I consolidated.

With that, I had the freedom to rebuild the site from scratch, and how I did it I think reflects how software is changing in the AI era. In many ways, I think software is following the same path as food and design - moving from mass-produced to custom.

---


A few years ago, I spent the day in the test kitchen and fermentation lab at Noma. That experience was formative for me. While a digital nomad, I had visited many of the top restaurants in the world. But, Noma's focus on craft seemed to be at another level. Noma had felt distinctive - local ingredients, unique fermentation approaches. Even the dining room featured handmade ceramics, all slightly different and unique. It felt quite different from traditional restaurants - and quite the opposite of [mass-industrialized factories](/rethinking-work-beyond-the-factory).

While reading [New Nordic Cuisine, Aesthetics, and Place: A Compendium](https://petermiller.com/products/new-nordic-cuisine-aesthetics-and-place-a-compendium-new-exhibit-arriving-in-seattle-november-15th)[^peter], this passage stood out to me:

> In many ways, the closing decades of the 21st century were all about having more of everything, a period marked by an aesthetics of abundance. In the private home, kitchens grew bigger and glistened with chrome and cool surfaces. Since then, things have changed. The glossy sheen of the mass-produced gave way to a desire for something warmer, for objects that radiate individuality, history and a sense of place. People wanted to signal their adherence to values such as sustainability and shared responsibility, but at the same time to mark themselves out as individuals.

As I read this passage, I realized that software has been following the same path.

Over the last decades, the economy made the transition from analog to digital. Schedules moved online, direct deposit replaced paper checks, and every restaurant got an iPad and a website.

To aid in that transition, many software factories were born. Salesforce, Kronos, Atlassian, and many others built tools that were adopted by millions of people. Customizing them for the specific workflows of each business was tedious, but the efficiency gains were significant. There was not enough talent for each business to build their own software, so they relied on economies of scale from big tech to get software for less than the cost of a single software developer. It was the "shiny kitchen" era of software.

Over time, people grew frustrated with the limitations of mass-produced software. It was hard to set up, companies spent a lot of money on agencies to customize it, and it could be tedious to learn. "Low-code" emerged as a stop-gap, but was still difficult.

Then, over the last year - [coding became "solved" by AI](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/head-of-claude-code-what-happens). LLMs passed the [Pareto frontier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_front) where humans could now build the software that they want.

While some believe that AI will kill the software industry, I disagree. I think AI is going to create a Cambrian explosion of new software - driven by small teams that want control and customization over their workflows.

There is latent demand for custom software. Some is regional - like ecommerce that integrates with [OXXO Pay](https://www.nuvei.com/posts/oxxo-pay), some is niche like [software for managing political campaigns](https://www.ngpvan.com/), and some is frontier like [self-driving cars](https://waymo.com/).

The industry is shifting from "Build software many people want" to "Build the software just for you and your team". And, the result is a new era of software - that is more niche, custom, and interesting.

And, to support this - the tech industry will change. The next wave of tech companies will be focused on toolmaking, rather than selling services.

---

I originally explored the idea of personal software in [An app of one's own](/app-of-ones-own). Since then, I have continued to build a lot of software for [myself](/trivet-adds-google-sign-in-to-ghost), [my software](/a-mini-data-center), and [my writing](/three-ring-binder). Over time, I felt constrained by the limits of mass-produced software and the overhead of building software for multiple people.

For the past few years, contraption.co has been built on the [Ghost](https://ghost.org/) blogging platform. I can develop themes on top of it, but lack low-level access to the code. I built a plugin for it called [Trivet](/trivet-adds-google-sign-in-to-ghost). But, as my development moved from hand-written to AI, I hit snags. Good AI development wants low-level code access, and it wants everything in one repo. As I wanted to build better search into the blog powered by [Chroma](https://trychroma.com), I found myself fighting the framework rather than being empowered by it. So, I ripped it out and rebuilt the website from scratch (with AI) using a coding framework rather than blogging framework.

My personal website philipithomas.com used to be built in [Postcard](/launching-postcard), which is a personal website building software I build, open-source, and operate. It was made for an era when making websites was hard, and to do that there is a lot of logic about authentication, authorization, multi-tenancy in a shared database, and data privacy. Well, for my new website - I thought from first principles about what I wanted, and I decided that because I was building this software for myself - I could replace my database with the local filesystem. It was a curiosity I explored in [Three-Ring Binder](/three-ring-binder). But, in many ways - you can simplify software if you are building it for yourself. And, I could lean into quirkiness like incorporating blog cover images into search.

Along the way, I found myself facing new challenges for AI-powered development. I decided to build email sending through a command-line interface, but had to figure out how to stop AI agents from accidentally triggering emails to thousands of people. The solution I found was to wrap all production commands in the 1Password CLI, so I would have to scan a human fingerprint to send emails.

So, my new website is live today - check it out on [philipithomas.com](https://www.philipithomas.com). The UI is a new project called [Bully Pulpit](https://github.com/philipithomas/bully-pulpit), and the backend newsletter software called [Printing Press](https://github.com/philipithomas/printing-press) - both open-source. The AI deep research agent is my favorite feature.

---

The original vision of Contraption Company when I set it up in January 2020 was to build multiple software products for other people. It was not really until last year, 2025, that I really hit a stride of being able to build multiple software products for other people. But, as the year progressed - it became clear that other people could build software themselves, too.

So, farewell to the Contraption Company brand, and onwards to the new software era.
